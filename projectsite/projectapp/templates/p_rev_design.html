The Process

Old -vs- New UI

To move the older Reputation and Surveys products onto the new UI with our Marketing and Engage products, we had to update the navigation, sitemap, and consolidate UI patterns. The Marketing/Engage track had been developed without full design involvement, so this project was an opportunity to lay down basic color, page layout guidelines, and make patterns consistent in the new UI as well.

Top image How the dashboard looked like on the Reputations and Surveys products.

Bottom image what the dashboard looked like on Marketing and Engage. Even though these products could accessed altogether, the interface for the dashboards were clearly very different.

Prioritizing & Planning

As design lead, I solidified the project prioritization and timeline with the lead FE engineer and our CPO after I inherited this project. I laid out our shared understanding of the entire process, what the timelines are, what state different parts of the UI were in, what items still needed to be tackled (and how to prioritize them) -- and finally, action items we needed to take care of.

We whiteboarded the order of steps for the project to get team consensus.
A sitemap and navigation re-organization since the navigation had originally grown very organically and was poorly laid out.

I led the charge to gather feedback from our Customer Success team to learn different use-cases, and worked closely with Product and Eng to determine the application sitemap and hierarchy.

Case Study: Dashboard

The first page a user lands on once they sign in is the dashboard. Both Reputation and Marketing had their dashboard layouts as well as their own specific kinds of modules. My first step to unifying the interface was to inventory all the different modules by layout, function, and which of the apps they appeared in.

After identifying shared attributes and potential for shared layouts, I mocked up what all the modules from the Reputation dashboard might look like using the visual pattern of the newer Revinate Marketing dashboard. A few tweaks would need to be made for both interfaces, but the translation was smooth.

Style Guide

The timing presented a great opportunity to establish a style guide in parallel. While unifying all the elements of the four applications, we also documented navigation patterns (treatments for primary, secondary, tertiary navigation), as well as color interaction rules, and repeated visual patterns.
